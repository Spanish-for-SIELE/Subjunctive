<h1>El Subjuntivo — Spanish Notes</h1>

<h2>1. When to Use the Subjunctive Mood</h2>
<ul>
  <li>To express <strong>wishes, desires, or hopes</strong> (e.g., "Espero que vengas").</li>
  <li>To show <strong>emotions</strong> (e.g., "Me alegra que estés aquí").</li>
  <li>To indicate <strong>doubt or uncertainty</strong> (e.g., "Dudo que funcione").</li>
  <li>To use <strong>impersonal expressions</strong> (e.g., "Es necesario que estudies").</li>
  <li>To make <strong>recommendations or give advice</strong> (e.g., "Te sugiero que leas esto").</li>
  <li>To express <strong>hypotheticals or unreal situations</strong>.</li>
</ul>

<h2>2. Present Subjunctive Conjugation Rules</h2>
<p>Start from the <strong>"yo" form of the present indicative</strong>, remove the <code>-o</code>, and add the following endings:</p>
<table>
  <thead>
    <tr>
      <th>Pronombre</th>
      <th>-ar verbs</th>
      <th>-er / -ir verbs</th>
    </tr>
  </thead>
  <tbody>
    <tr><td>yo</td><td>-e</td><td>-a</td></tr>
    <tr><td>tú</td><td>-es</td><td>-as</td></tr>
    <tr><td>él/ella/usted</td><td>-e</td><td>-a</td></tr>
    <tr><td>nosotros/as</td><td>-emos</td><td>-amos</td></tr>
    <tr><td>ellos/ellas/ustedes</td><td>-en</td><td>-an</td></tr>
  </tbody>
</table>

<h2>4. Triggers of the Subjunctive</h2>
<p>The subjunctive often follows specific expressions or conjunctions:</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Wishes:</strong> querer que, esperar que, desear que</li>
  <li><strong>Emotions:</strong> temer que, alegrarse de que, sorprenderse de que</li>
  <li><strong>Doubt/Negation:</strong> dudar que, no creer que, negar que</li>
  <li><strong>Impersonal Expressions:</strong> es importante que, es posible que</li>
  <li><strong>Conjunctions:</strong> para que, a menos que, antes de que, con tal de que</li>
</ul>

<h2>5. Past Subjunctive (Imperfect Subjunctive) – Notes</h2>
<p>The <strong>past subjunctive</strong> (also known as the imperfect subjunctive) is used to express doubt, emotion, desire, or hypothetical situations <strong>in the past</strong>.</p>

<h3>When to Use the Past Subjunctive</h3>
<ul>
  <li>After verbs in the past or conditional tense that trigger the subjunctive</li>
  <li>For hypothetical or contrary-to-fact situations ("if" clauses)</li>
  <li>To make polite requests or wishes</li>
</ul>

<table>
  <thead>
    <tr><th>English</th><th>Spanish Example</th></tr>
  </thead>
  <tbody>
    <tr><td>If I were rich...</td><td>Si yo <strong>fuera</strong> rico...</td></tr>
    <tr><td>I wanted you to come.</td><td>Quería que tú <strong>vinieras</strong>.</td></tr>
    <tr><td>I'd like you to help.</td><td>Quisiera que me <strong>ayudaras</strong>.</td></tr>
    <tr><td>I doubted he knew it.</td><td>Dudaba que él lo <strong>supiera</strong>.</td></tr>
  </tbody>
</table>

<h2>7. The two imperfect-subjunctive forms: <code>-ra</code> vs <code>-se</code></h2>
<p>Every imperfect subjunctive has <strong>two interchangeable forms</strong>. Both come from the 3rd-person plural preterite minus <code>-ron</code>.</p>
<table>
  <thead>
    <tr><th>Pronombre</th><th>-ra form</th><th>-se form</th></tr>
  </thead>
  <tbody>
    <tr><td>yo</td><td>hablara</td><td>hablase</td></tr>
    <tr><td>tú</td><td>hablaras</td><td>hablases</td></tr>
    <tr><td>él/ella/usted</td><td>hablara</td><td>hablase</td></tr>
    <tr><td>nosotros/as</td><td>habláramos</td><td>hablásemos</td></tr>
    <tr><td>ellos/ellas/ustedes</td><td>hablaran</td><td>hablasen</td></tr>
  </tbody>
</table>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Mostly interchangeable:</strong> <em>Si tuviera/tuviese tiempo…</em> — both correct.</li>
  <li><strong>Register:</strong> the <code>-se</code> form sounds slightly more formal/literary and is more common in Spain in writing. The <code>-ra</code> form dominates speech everywhere and in Latin America.</li>
  <li><strong>Exception — only <code>-ra</code> works</strong> in a few uses: the "softened" conditional (<em>Quisiera un café; Debiera estudiar más; Más valiera callar</em>) and the literary pluperfect-for-preterite (<em>el hombre que <strong>llegara</strong> ayer</em> = que había llegado). Don't use <code>-se</code> there.</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>C1 takeaway:</strong> being able to produce <em>and recognise</em> both forms is expected. Pick one and stay consistent within a sentence.</p>

<h2>8. Sequence of Tenses (Correlación / Consecutio Temporum)</h2>
<p>This is the single most important C1 subjunctive skill: the tense of the <strong>main verb</strong> decides which subjunctive you may use in the subordinate clause.</p>

<table>
  <thead>
    <tr><th>Main clause (indicative)</th><th>Subordinate (subjunctive)</th><th>Example</th></tr>
  </thead>
  <tbody>
    <tr>
      <td>Present / Future / Imperative / Present perfect</td>
      <td>Present subj. <em>(same or later time)</em><br>Present perfect subj. <em>(earlier, completed)</em></td>
      <td>Quiero que <strong>vengas</strong>.<br>Me alegro de que <strong>hayas venido</strong>.</td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>Preterite / Imperfect / Conditional / Pluperfect</td>
      <td>Imperfect subj. <em>(same or later time)</em><br>Pluperfect subj. <em>(earlier, completed)</em></td>
      <td>Quería que <strong>vinieras</strong>.<br>Me alegré de que <strong>hubieras venido</strong>.</td>
    </tr>
  </tbody>
</table>

<p><strong>Rule of thumb:</strong> present-zone main verb → present-zone subjunctive; past-zone main verb → past-zone subjunctive.</p>
<p><strong>Common C1 error to avoid:</strong> <em>«Me dijo que <span style="text-decoration:line-through">venga</span>»</em> ✗ → <em>«Me dijo que <strong>viniera</strong>»</em> ✓. A past reporting verb forces the past subjunctive.</p>
<p><strong>Subtle but allowed:</strong> when the subordinate idea is still true now, a present subjunctive can survive after a past verb — <em>«Te dije que <strong>tengas</strong> cuidado»</em> (the warning still stands). Both <em>tengas</em> and <em>tuvieras</em> are defensible; know why.</p>

<h2>9. Indicative vs. Subjunctive — the decisions that separate B2 from C1</h2>
<p>The core principle: <strong>subjunctive = the speaker is not asserting the clause as a real, known fact</strong> (it's wished, doubted, evaluated, hypothetical, or not-yet-real). Indicative = asserted as real/known.</p>

<h3>(a) Opinion & mental verbs — flip on negation</h3>
<table>
  <thead><tr><th>Affirmative → indicative</th><th>Negative → subjunctive</th></tr></thead>
  <tbody>
    <tr><td>Creo que <strong>es</strong> verdad.</td><td>No creo que <strong>sea</strong> verdad.</td></tr>
    <tr><td>Está claro que <strong>viene</strong>.</td><td>No está claro que <strong>venga</strong>.</td></tr>
    <tr><td>Pienso que <strong>tiene</strong> razón.</td><td>No pienso que <strong>tenga</strong> razón.</td></tr>
  </tbody>
</table>
<p>With <strong>quizá(s) / tal vez / posiblemente</strong>, either mood works — subjunctive expresses more doubt: <em>Quizá <strong>venga</strong></em> (less sure) vs <em>Quizá <strong>viene</strong></em> (more sure). <strong>A lo mejor</strong> always takes indicative.</p>

<h3>(b) "El hecho de que" and fronted clauses</h3>
<ul>
  <li><em>El hecho de que <strong>llegaras</strong> tarde molestó a todos.</em> — a clause that <em>evaluates/comments</em> on a fact → subjunctive, even though the fact is real.</li>
  <li><em><strong>Que</strong> no <strong>haya</strong> dinero no significa que no <strong>podamos</strong> intentarlo.</em></li>
  <li><strong>No es que</strong> + subjunctive (rejecting an explanation): <em>No es que no <strong>quiera</strong>, es que no <strong>puedo</strong>.</em></li>
  <li><strong>No porque</strong> + subjunctive: <em>Lo hago no porque <strong>tenga</strong> que hacerlo, sino porque quiero.</em></li>
</ul>

<h3>(c) Adjective (relative) clauses — known vs. unknown/non-existent antecedent</h3>
<table>
  <thead><tr><th>Antecedent is specific/known → indicative</th><th>Antecedent is indefinite, sought, or denied → subjunctive</th></tr></thead>
  <tbody>
    <tr><td>Tengo un coche que <strong>corre</strong> mucho.</td><td>Busco un coche que <strong>corra</strong> mucho.</td></tr>
    <tr><td>Conozco a alguien que <strong>habla</strong> ruso.</td><td>No conozco a nadie que <strong>hable</strong> ruso.</td></tr>
    <tr><td>Hay una sala que <strong>está</strong> libre.</td><td>¿Hay alguna sala que <strong>esté</strong> libre?</td></tr>
  </tbody>
</table>

<h3>(d) Temporal clauses — future reference triggers subjunctive</h3>
<p>After <strong>cuando, en cuanto, tan pronto como, hasta que, mientras, una vez que, después de que</strong>: use the <strong>subjunctive when the action hasn't happened yet</strong> (future), indicative when it's habitual or past.</p>
<table>
  <thead><tr><th>Not yet happened → subjunctive</th><th>Habitual / past fact → indicative</th></tr></thead>
  <tbody>
    <tr><td>Cuando <strong>llegues</strong>, llámame.</td><td>Cuando <strong>llego</strong> a casa, ceno. (habit)</td></tr>
    <tr><td>En cuanto <strong>termine</strong>, salgo.</td><td>En cuanto <strong>terminé</strong>, salí. (past)</td></tr>
    <tr><td>Esperaré hasta que <strong>vuelvas</strong>.</td><td>Esperé hasta que <strong>volviste</strong>.</td></tr>
  </tbody>
</table>
<p><strong>Antes de que</strong> is special — it <em>always</em> takes subjunctive: <em>Antes de que <strong>te vayas</strong>…</em></p>

<h3>(e) Concessive clauses — <em>aunque</em> & co.</h3>
<table>
  <thead><tr><th>Indicative = real / conceded fact</th><th>Subjunctive = hypothetical or "even if / I don't care"</th></tr></thead>
  <tbody>
    <tr><td>Aunque <strong>llueve</strong>, salgo. (it IS raining)</td><td>Aunque <strong>llueva</strong>, saldré. (even if it rains)</td></tr>
    <tr><td>Aunque <strong>era</strong> caro, lo compré.</td><td>Aunque <strong>fuera</strong> caro, lo compraría.</td></tr>
  </tbody>
</table>
<p>Also subjunctive: <strong>por más que / por mucho que / por muy + adj. + que</strong> (however much…): <em>Por mucho que <strong>insistas</strong>, no iré. Por muy listo que <strong>sea</strong>, se equivoca.</em></p>

<h3>(f) Purpose vs. result, and other fixed-subjunctive conjunctions</h3>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Always + subjunctive:</strong> para que, a fin de que, a menos que, a no ser que, con tal de que, en caso de que, sin que, antes de que.</li>
  <li><em>Te lo explico <strong>para que</strong> lo <strong>entiendas</strong>. Saldremos <strong>a menos que</strong> <strong>llueva</strong>. Lo hizo <strong>sin que</strong> nadie se <strong>diera</strong> cuenta.</em></li>
  <li><strong>de manera/modo que</strong>: subjunctive = purpose (<em>so that</em>), indicative = result (<em>and so</em>). <em>Habla claro de modo que te <strong>entiendan</strong></em> (purpose) vs <em>Habló claro, de modo que todos lo <strong>entendieron</strong></em> (result).</li>
</ul>

<h3>(g) <em>Ojalá</em> across the time-line (a compact subjunctive map)</h3>
<table>
  <thead><tr><th>Form</th><th>Meaning</th></tr></thead>
  <tbody>
    <tr><td>Ojalá <strong>venga</strong>.</td><td>I hope he comes (still possible).</td></tr>
    <tr><td>Ojalá <strong>viniera/viniese</strong>.</td><td>I wish he would come (unlikely / not happening).</td></tr>
    <tr><td>Ojalá <strong>haya venido</strong>.</td><td>I hope he has come (maybe already did).</td></tr>
    <tr><td>Ojalá <strong>hubiera/hubiese venido</strong>.</td><td>I wish he had come (but he didn't — regret).</td></tr>
  </tbody>
</table>

<h2>10. SIELE / C1 traps & quick self-check</h2>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Same subject → infinitive, not <code>que</code>:</strong> <em>Quiero <strong>ir</strong></em> (not «quiero que vaya»). <em>Espero <strong>aprobar</strong>.</em> Use <code>que</code> + subjunctive only when the two subjects differ.</li>
  <li><strong>Verbs of communication/perception are dual:</strong> <em>Dice que <strong>viene</strong></em> (reporting a fact = indicative) vs <em>Dice que <strong>vengas</strong></em> (relaying an order = subjunctive). Same with <em>insistir en, repetir</em>.</li>
  <li><strong>Emotion about a real fact still takes subjunctive:</strong> <em>Me molesta que <strong>fumes</strong></em> — even though you do smoke.</li>
  <li><strong>Hasta que no:</strong> the "no" is expletive (not really negative): <em>No saldré hasta que (no) <strong>termines</strong>.</em></li>
  <li><strong>Don't over-trigger:</strong> <em>porque, ya que, puesto que, como (= since)</em> take <strong>indicative</strong> — they state real causes.</li>
  <li>See <a href="../Conditional-Tense/">Conditional</a> and the <a href="../Si-Clauses/">Si-Clauses</a> note for hypothetical <code>si</code> sentences, the other big subjunctive arena.</li>
</ul>
